Cost Segregation & Depreciation

A cost segregation study is an IRS-approved, engineering-based analysis that reclassifies components of a building from the default 27.5-year (residential) or 39-year (commercial) depreciation schedule into accelerated recovery periods of 5, 7, or 15 years under the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS). Items such as cabinetry, flooring, specialty electrical, plumbing fixtures, landscaping, and site improvements are identified and reassigned. Under IRC Section 168(k), qualifying components are eligible for bonus depreciation, which under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) is now permanently set at 100%. This means the reclassified portions can be fully expensed in Year 1 rather than depreciated over decades. For a typical $500,000 rental property, a cost segregation study may reclassify 25% to 40% of the building cost into accelerated categories, generating $125,000 to $200,000 in first-year deductions that offset taxable income from rents, business operations, or other qualifying sources.

The cost of a cost segregation study varies based on property type, size, and complexity, but typically ranges from $3,000 to $15,000 for most residential and small commercial properties. Larger or more complex properties such as hotels, medical facilities, or multi-building portfolios may be higher. In virtually every case we have worked on, the tax savings generated in the first year alone have exceeded the cost of the study by a factor of 10x to 50x. For example, a $6,000 study on a $750,000 short-term rental property routinely produces $80,000 to $150,000 in accelerated first-year deductions. The study fee is itself a deductible business expense under IRC Section 162, and the resulting tax savings recur for the life of the property through the reclassified depreciation schedules.

Yes. A cost segregation study can be performed on any property you currently own, regardless of when it was acquired. If you have been depreciating the building using the standard straight-line method, the study will reclassify eligible components into shorter recovery periods retroactively. The resulting catch-up depreciation is claimed through IRS Form 3115 (Application for Change in Accounting Method), which allows you to take a single cumulative adjustment in the current tax year for all the accelerated depreciation you would have claimed had the study been performed at purchase. This is a powerful mechanism because it does not require amending prior-year returns. For properties owned for several years, the catch-up deduction under Section 481(a) can be substantial, and we have routinely seen single-year adjustments exceeding $100,000 to $200,000 for clients who purchased properties five or more years ago.

Bonus depreciation under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law in 2025, permanently restores 100% first-year bonus depreciation for qualifying property under IRC Section 168(k). This reverses the phasedown that had been scheduled under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, which reduced bonus depreciation to 80% in 2023, 60% in 2024, and so on. With the OBBBA, assets placed in service with recovery periods of 20 years or less, including 5-year, 7-year, and 15-year property identified through cost segregation studies, can be fully expensed in the year they are placed in service. For real estate investors, this means that when a cost segregation study reclassifies building components into these shorter-lived categories, the entire reclassified amount is deductible in Year 1. This is one of the most significant tax benefits available to property owners today, and it applies to both newly acquired and previously owned properties when claimed via Form 3115.

The core engineering methodology of a cost segregation study is the same for both property types, but the tax treatment differs significantly based on the rental classification and the owner's participation. Short-term rentals (average guest stay of 7 days or fewer) classified under IRC Section 469(c)(7) and Reg. 1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii) are treated as non-passive activities when the owner materially participates, meaning the accelerated depreciation deductions can offset W-2 wages, business income, and other active income without limitation. Long-term rentals default to passive activity status under Section 469, which generally limits loss deductions to passive income unless the taxpayer qualifies as a Real Estate Professional under Section 469(c)(7). Additionally, short-term rentals use a 39-year base recovery period (as lodging, not residential rental), while long-term residential rentals use 27.5 years, which affects the portion eligible for reclassification. Both property types benefit substantially from cost segregation, but the ability to use the resulting losses against active income is what makes STR cost segregation particularly powerful for business owners with high W-2 or active business income.

Savings from a cost segregation study depend on the property's purchase price, the percentage of components eligible for reclassification, your marginal tax rate, and whether the resulting losses can offset active or passive income. As a general benchmark, a cost segregation study typically reclassifies 25% to 40% of a building's depreciable basis into accelerated categories. On a $600,000 property with $500,000 in depreciable basis, that translates to $125,000 to $200,000 in first-year deductions. At a combined federal and state marginal tax rate of 37%, the cash tax savings in Year 1 would be approximately $46,000 to $74,000. We have completed studies for clients where the first-year tax savings exceeded $150,000 on a single property, particularly for short-term rental owners who qualify for material participation and can apply the losses against their active business income.

S-Corp & Entity Structure

An S-Corporation election under IRC Section 1362 can be one of the most effective tax-reduction strategies for business owners earning consistent net profit above approximately $60,000 to $80,000 per year. The primary benefit is reducing self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare) by splitting business income into two components: a reasonable salary subject to payroll taxes, and distributions that are not subject to self-employment tax. For a business owner earning $250,000 in net profit, the S-Corp election can save $15,000 to $25,000 annually in employment taxes alone. However, the election adds compliance requirements including payroll processing, reasonable compensation documentation, and separate tax filings (Form 1120-S). It is not beneficial for every situation, particularly businesses with inconsistent income, significant losses, or owners who plan to retain substantial earnings in the entity. A proper analysis of your income level, growth trajectory, and state tax implications is essential before filing Form 2553.

Reasonable officer compensation is the salary amount that an S-Corp shareholder-employee must pay themselves, and it is one of the most scrutinized areas by the IRS in S-Corporation audits. Under IRC Section 3121(a) and Treasury Regulation 31.3121(a)-1, shareholder-employees who provide services to the corporation must receive compensation that reflects what the open market would pay for equivalent services. Factors the IRS considers include the officer's qualifications and experience, the nature and scope of work performed, comparable salaries in similar industries and geographic areas, and the corporation's revenue and profitability. Setting compensation too low triggers IRS reclassification of distributions as wages, resulting in back taxes, penalties, and interest. We prepare formal reasonable compensation analyses for every S-Corp client, documenting comparable salary data and the specific duties performed to create an audit-defensible position. In our experience, reasonable compensation typically falls between 40% and 60% of the business's net profit, though this varies significantly by industry and role.

The Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction under IRC Section 199A allows eligible S-Corp owners to deduct up to 20% of their qualified business income from their personal tax return, effectively reducing the tax rate on pass-through business income. However, the deduction is subject to limitations based on taxable income, the type of business, and W-2 wages paid by the entity. For specified service trades or businesses (SSTBs), including law, accounting, health, consulting, and financial services, the deduction phases out entirely when taxable income exceeds $243,125 for single filers or $486,250 for joint filers (2026 thresholds, indexed for inflation). For non-service businesses above the income threshold, the deduction is limited to the greater of 50% of W-2 wages paid or 25% of W-2 wages plus 2.5% of the unadjusted basis of qualified property. This creates an important interplay with S-Corp compensation planning: increasing officer salary boosts the W-2 wage base, which can increase the QBI deduction, partially or fully offsetting the additional payroll tax cost. We model these tradeoffs for every S-Corp client.

The fundamental difference is how the entity's income is taxed. An S-Corporation is a pass-through entity under Subchapter S of the Internal Revenue Code: the business itself pays no federal income tax, and all income, deductions, and credits flow through to the shareholders' personal returns. A C-Corporation under Subchapter C is taxed at the entity level at a flat 21% federal rate, and distributions to shareholders are taxed again as dividends, creating so-called double taxation. However, the C-Corp structure offers advantages in specific situations: the flat 21% rate can be lower than the owner's marginal personal rate (which can reach 37% federally), retained earnings grow inside the entity at the lower rate, and C-Corps can provide tax-free fringe benefits to shareholder-employees under IRC Section 162 that are not available in S-Corps, including employer-paid health insurance premiums, group term life insurance, and educational assistance programs. Many of our clients operate both an S-Corp for active business income and a C-Corp for benefit planning and retained earnings strategies.

A C-Corporation election is most beneficial when a business owner plans to retain significant earnings inside the entity rather than distributing them, when the owner's personal marginal tax rate exceeds 21%, or when the business would benefit from C-Corp-exclusive fringe benefits. Under IRC Section 11, C-Corps pay a flat 21% federal tax rate on retained earnings, compared to potential personal rates of 32% to 37% for high-income S-Corp owners. C-Corps also qualify for tax-free fringe benefits under Section 162, including employer-paid health insurance, group term life up to $50,000, dependent care assistance, and educational assistance up to $5,250 per year. Additionally, C-Corp stock may qualify for the Section 1202 Qualified Small Business Stock exclusion, which can exempt up to $10 million in capital gains from federal tax upon sale. We have implemented C-Corp strategies for business owners who were retaining $200,000 or more annually, resulting in immediate tax rate reductions of 10 to 16 percentage points on retained income.

Real Estate Tax Strategies

Short-term rental owners can reduce taxes significantly by qualifying for material participation, which reclassifies rental losses from passive to non-passive, allowing those losses to offset W-2 wages, business income, and other active income without limitation. Under Treasury Regulation 1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii), a rental activity with an average customer use period of 7 days or less is not treated as a rental activity for passive loss purposes. If the owner then materially participates by meeting one of the seven tests in Reg. 1.469-5T, typically by spending more than 500 hours or more than any other individual on the activity, the resulting losses become fully deductible against all income types. When combined with a cost segregation study and 100% bonus depreciation under the OBBBA, a short-term rental owner can generate $100,000 or more in paper losses in Year 1 that directly reduce their W-2 or business tax liability. This strategy has become one of the most powerful legal tax reduction tools available, and we have implemented it for dozens of clients with documented tax savings exceeding $50,000 per property in the first year.

The Augusta Rule, codified in IRC Section 280A(g), allows a homeowner to rent their personal residence for up to 14 days per year without reporting the rental income on their tax return. For business owners, this creates a planning opportunity: the business can rent the owner's home for legitimate business purposes such as board meetings, team retreats, client events, or strategic planning sessions, and deduct the rental payment as an ordinary business expense under Section 162, while the owner receives the income completely tax-free. The rental rate must reflect fair market value for comparable event or meeting space in the area, and the business use must be bona fide with documented agendas, attendee lists, and business purpose. When properly documented, this strategy can shift $5,000 to $20,000 or more per year in tax-free income to the business owner while generating a corresponding business deduction. We help clients establish the required documentation framework, obtain comparable rental rate analyses, and maintain compliant records.

It depends on your level of participation and the type of rental activity. Under IRC Section 469, rental activities are generally classified as passive, meaning losses can only offset other passive income. However, there are important exceptions. First, taxpayers with adjusted gross income under $100,000 may deduct up to $25,000 in rental losses against active income under the Section 469(i) special allowance, which phases out completely at $150,000 AGI. Second, taxpayers who qualify as Real Estate Professionals under Section 469(c)(7) by spending more than 750 hours and more than half their working time in real property trades or businesses can treat rental losses as non-passive. Third, short-term rental properties with average guest stays of 7 days or fewer are excluded from the passive rental definition under Reg. 1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii), and when the owner materially participates, the losses are fully deductible against W-2 and business income. This third exception is particularly powerful when combined with cost segregation and bonus depreciation.

A 1031 exchange, named after IRC Section 1031, allows a real estate investor to defer capital gains taxes when selling an investment property by reinvesting the proceeds into a like-kind replacement property. The exchange must meet strict timing requirements: the replacement property must be identified within 45 days of closing on the relinquished property, and the acquisition must be completed within 180 days. A qualified intermediary must hold the funds during the exchange period, as the seller cannot take constructive receipt of the proceeds. The properties must be held for investment or productive use in a trade or business, and personal residences do not qualify. A 1031 exchange is most valuable when you have significant built-in capital gains, when you want to reposition into a higher-performing asset, or when you are consolidating or diversifying your real estate portfolio. We coordinate 1031 exchanges with cost segregation studies on the replacement property to maximize both the deferral of gains on the sale and the acceleration of depreciation on the new acquisition.

The state and local tax (SALT) deduction cap, established by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act at $10,000 per return and extended under the OBBBA at a higher threshold, limits the amount of state income taxes and local property taxes that can be deducted on Schedule A of a personal federal return. For real estate investors with significant property tax bills across multiple holdings, this cap can result in substantial lost deductions. However, the cap applies only to taxes paid at the individual level. Property taxes on rental real estate are deductible as business expenses on Schedule E and are not subject to the SALT cap. Similarly, state income taxes paid through a pass-through entity tax (PTET) election, where available, are deducted at the entity level and bypass the individual SALT limitation. We work with investors in PTET-eligible states to ensure they are taking full advantage of this workaround, and we structure property ownership to maximize the deductibility of property taxes as business rather than personal expenses.

The Georgia Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) election allows S-Corporations, partnerships, and LLCs taxed as partnerships to elect to pay Georgia state income tax at the entity level rather than passing the tax obligation through to individual owners. Under Georgia Code Section 48-7-21.1, the entity pays a 5.39% tax on its Georgia-source income, and the owners receive a corresponding credit on their personal Georgia returns. The critical benefit is that the state tax paid at the entity level is deductible as a business expense on the federal return, effectively bypassing the $10,000 individual SALT deduction cap imposed by the TCJA. For a Georgia business owner with $500,000 in pass-through income, the PTET election can recover approximately $20,000 or more in federal deductions that would otherwise be lost to the SALT cap. Over 30 states now offer some form of PTET election, and we evaluate and implement this strategy for every client in an eligible state.

A CPA (Certified Public Accountant) is a licensed professional who typically focuses on tax preparation, compliance, bookkeeping, and financial statement audits. Most CPAs work reactively, processing completed transactions and filing accurate returns based on what has already happened. A tax strategist works proactively, analyzing your financial situation before taxable events occur and designing strategies that legally minimize your tax liability going forward. Tax strategists focus on entity structuring, income timing, depreciation optimization, retirement plan design, and coordinated multi-year planning. The distinction matters because tax preparation and tax planning are fundamentally different services. Filing a return correctly does not mean the underlying transactions were structured to minimize taxes. Our firm works alongside your existing CPA to implement strategies they can then reflect on your returns.

A 3-year lookback analysis is a comprehensive review of your last three years of filed tax returns designed to identify missed deductions, overlooked credits, structural inefficiencies, and tax planning opportunities that were not captured during the original preparation. This is the starting point for every AE Tax Advisors engagement. During the lookback, our team examines entity classification, depreciation schedules, retirement contributions, business expense categorization, state tax elections, and dozens of other line items across your personal and business returns. Common findings include missed depreciation on rental properties, failure to elect S-Corp status at the optimal time, underutilized retirement plan contributions, overlooked home office deductions, and unclaimed business credits. When the lookback reveals recoverable savings on prior returns, we file amended returns (Form 1040-X) or Form 3115 to capture those savings retroactively. The lookback consistently uncovers $10,000 to $50,000 in recoverable tax savings from prior years.

If your previously filed tax returns contain missed deductions, overlooked credits, or incorrect reporting, you can file an amended return using IRS Form 1040-X to correct the errors and claim the refund. Under IRC Section 6511, you generally have three years from the date the original return was filed (or two years from the date the tax was paid, whichever is later) to file an amended return and claim a refund. For depreciation-related corrections, Form 3115 (Application for Change in Accounting Method) may be used instead, which allows a cumulative catch-up adjustment in the current year without amending each prior year individually. This is particularly advantageous for missed cost segregation deductions. We have filed amended returns for clients recovering $15,000 to $80,000 in refunds from a single prior year, and Form 3115 adjustments that produced current-year catch-up deductions exceeding $200,000.

Under IRC Section 6511(a), the general statute of limitations for filing an amended return and claiming a refund is three years from the date the original return was filed, or two years from the date the tax was paid, whichever period expires later. For most taxpayers who file on time and pay with their return, this means you can amend returns for the prior three tax years. However, there are important exceptions: if you filed an extension, the three-year period runs from the extended filing date; if you had a net operating loss (NOL) carryback, special rules under Section 6511(d) may extend the period; and for depreciation corrections, Form 3115 allows you to capture all missed depreciation in the current year regardless of how far back the error originated. This is why the Form 3115 approach is so powerful for cost segregation catch-up deductions on properties purchased five, ten, or even twenty years ago.

An accountable plan is a formal reimbursement arrangement under IRC Section 62(c) and Treasury Regulation 1.62-2 that allows a business to reimburse employees (including shareholder-employees of an S-Corp) for legitimate business expenses on a tax-free basis. The reimbursements are deductible by the business and excluded from the employee's taxable income and W-2 wages, meaning neither income tax nor employment tax applies. To qualify, the plan must meet three requirements: the expenses must have a business connection, the employee must adequately account for expenses within a reasonable time (typically 60 days), and any excess reimbursement must be returned within a reasonable time (typically 120 days). Common reimbursable expenses include home office costs, cell phone and internet, vehicle mileage, travel, professional development, and business-use equipment. Without a written accountable plan, these same reimbursements are treated as taxable compensation. For S-Corp owners, a properly implemented accountable plan can shift $10,000 to $30,000 in annual expenses from after-tax personal costs to pre-tax business deductions.

A Medical Expense Reimbursement Plan (MERP), authorized under IRC Section 105(b), is an employer-sponsored arrangement that reimburses employees for qualifying medical expenses on a pre-tax basis. For C-Corporation shareholder-employees, a MERP allows the corporation to deduct the reimbursed medical costs as a business expense under Section 162, while the employee receives the reimbursement completely tax-free. Qualifying expenses include health insurance premiums, dental and vision care, prescription medications, copays, deductibles, and other out-of-pocket medical costs for the employee, their spouse, and dependents. A properly structured MERP can reimburse $20,000 to $50,000 or more annually in medical expenses that the employee would otherwise pay with after-tax dollars. This is one of the key advantages of operating a C-Corporation for fringe benefit planning, as the same arrangement in an S-Corp would result in the reimbursements being included in the shareholder-employee's taxable wages.

Business Owner Deductions

An accountable plan under IRC Section 62(c) allows your business to reimburse you tax-free for any expense that has a legitimate business connection and is properly documented. Common deductible expenses include home office costs (calculated using the actual expense method for the business-use percentage of rent or mortgage interest, utilities, insurance, and maintenance), cell phone and internet bills (business-use percentage), vehicle expenses (either actual costs or the IRS standard mileage rate), business travel including airfare, lodging, and meals (at 50% for meals under Section 274), professional development and continuing education, business-use computers and equipment, professional subscriptions and memberships, and client entertainment costs where applicable. The key requirement is that each expense must be substantiated with receipts and a clear business purpose within 60 days of being incurred. We set up the written plan, documentation templates, and reimbursement procedures for every S-Corp client.

Yes, but the method matters. As an S-Corp shareholder-employee, you cannot claim the home office deduction directly on your personal return because the deduction under IRC Section 280A applies to self-employed individuals, not W-2 employees. Instead, the S-Corp should reimburse you for home office expenses through an accountable plan. The corporation calculates the business-use percentage of your home (based on dedicated square footage divided by total square footage) and reimburses you for that portion of rent or mortgage interest, property taxes, utilities, insurance, repairs, and depreciation. The S-Corp deducts the reimbursement as an ordinary business expense, and you receive it tax-free with no income or employment tax implications. This approach is actually more advantageous than the personal deduction because it also reduces your self-employment tax base. We prepare the accountable plan documentation and calculate the reimbursable amount for each client annually.

Hiring your children in your business is a legitimate tax strategy supported by IRC Section 3121(b)(3)(A), which exempts wages paid by a sole proprietorship or partnership (where each partner is a parent) to a child under age 18 from Social Security and Medicare taxes. If the child is under 21, the wages are also exempt from federal unemployment tax (FUTA). The child must perform real, age-appropriate work such as filing, cleaning, data entry, social media management, or assisting with marketing materials, and must be paid a reasonable rate for the work performed. For 2026, each child can earn up to the standard deduction amount (approximately $15,000) and pay zero federal income tax, while the business deducts the full amount as a wage expense. The child can then contribute their earnings to a Roth IRA, starting tax-free compound growth decades early. For a business owner in the 37% bracket, paying two children $14,000 each shifts $28,000 from the parent's taxable income to the children's zero-tax bracket, saving approximately $10,000 or more in combined taxes annually.

The Research and Development (R&D) tax credit under IRC Section 41 provides a dollar-for-dollar reduction in federal tax liability for businesses that engage in qualified research activities. Contrary to common misconception, the credit is not limited to laboratory research or technology companies. Any business that develops or improves products, processes, software, formulas, techniques, or inventions may qualify. The IRS applies a four-part test: the activity must be technological in nature, intended to develop a new or improved business component, involve elimination of uncertainty, and involve a process of experimentation. Qualifying expenses include wages for employees performing or supervising research, supplies used in research, and contract research expenses. Small businesses with gross receipts under $5 million can apply up to $250,000 of the credit against payroll taxes under Section 41(h), making it valuable even for pre-profit companies. We evaluate R&D credit eligibility for every business client, and we have identified qualifying activities in industries including manufacturing, construction, software development, food and beverage, and agriculture.

Estimated tax payments are quarterly installments required under IRC Section 6654 for taxpayers who expect to owe $1,000 or more in federal tax after subtracting withholding and credits. Payments are due April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15 of the following year. To avoid the underpayment penalty, you must pay at least 90% of the current year's tax liability or 100% of the prior year's liability (110% if your prior-year AGI exceeded $150,000) through a combination of withholding and estimated payments. The penalty is calculated on a quarter-by-quarter basis, so even if you pay enough in total for the year, uneven payments can trigger penalties on individual quarters. For business owners with variable income, we prepare quarterly tax projections that incorporate year-to-date income, expected remaining income, and all planned deductions to calculate the precise estimated payment needed each quarter. This prevents both underpayment penalties and the cash flow drag of overpaying estimates.

Retirement & Wealth Building

A backdoor Roth IRA is a two-step strategy that allows high-income earners who exceed the Roth IRA income limits to still contribute to a Roth IRA. Under IRC Section 408A, direct Roth IRA contributions phase out at modified AGI of $240,000 to $250,000 for joint filers in 2026. The backdoor strategy works by first making a non-deductible contribution to a traditional IRA (which has no income limit for contributions), then converting that traditional IRA to a Roth IRA. Since the contribution was made with after-tax dollars, only the earnings (if any) between contribution and conversion are taxable. If executed promptly, the taxable amount is minimal or zero. The critical consideration is the pro-rata rule under Section 408(d)(2): if you have existing pre-tax IRA balances, the conversion is taxed proportionally based on the ratio of pre-tax to after-tax funds across all your IRAs. We help clients clear pre-tax IRA balances through 401(k) rollovers before executing the backdoor conversion to avoid unexpected tax consequences.

A mega backdoor Roth is an advanced retirement strategy that allows eligible participants to contribute up to $46,000 beyond the standard 401(k) employee deferral limit by making after-tax (non-Roth) contributions to a 401(k) plan and then converting those contributions to a Roth account. The total 401(k) contribution limit for 2026 is $70,000 (employee plus employer contributions, with an additional catch-up for those 50 and over). After maximizing the standard employee deferral of $23,500 and receiving any employer match, the remaining room up to the $70,000 total limit can be filled with after-tax contributions if the plan permits. Those after-tax contributions are then converted to Roth either within the plan (in-plan Roth conversion) or rolled out to a Roth IRA (in-service distribution). This strategy is only available in plans that allow after-tax contributions and in-service distributions or in-plan conversions. For business owners who control their own Solo 401(k) plans, we design the plan document to include these provisions from the start.

For most self-employed business owners and S-Corp shareholder-employees, a Solo 401(k) is the superior choice over a SEP-IRA. Both plans allow employer contributions of up to 25% of compensation, but a Solo 401(k) also permits an employee elective deferral of up to $23,500 (2026 limit, plus $7,500 catch-up if age 50 or over), which the SEP-IRA does not. This means a Solo 401(k) participant can shelter significantly more income at lower compensation levels. For example, on $100,000 of W-2 compensation from an S-Corp, a Solo 401(k) allows $23,500 in employee deferrals plus $25,000 in employer contributions ($48,500 total), while a SEP-IRA would only allow $25,000. Additionally, Solo 401(k) plans can include a Roth contribution option, permit participant loans, and be designed to allow after-tax contributions for mega backdoor Roth conversions. The primary limitation is that Solo 401(k) plans are only available for businesses with no full-time employees other than the owner and spouse. We recommend SEP-IRAs primarily for businesses with employees where 401(k) administration costs are a concern.

A Health Savings Account (HSA) under IRC Section 223 is the only account in the tax code that offers a triple tax benefit: contributions are tax-deductible (or pre-tax if made through payroll), growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. For 2026, the contribution limits are $4,300 for individual coverage and $8,550 for family coverage, with a $1,000 catch-up for those 55 and over. To be eligible, you must be enrolled in a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). The retirement planning power of an HSA comes from treating it as a long-term investment account: rather than using the HSA to pay current medical bills, you pay medical expenses out of pocket, invest the HSA funds in growth-oriented assets, and let the account compound tax-free for decades. After age 65, HSA withdrawals for non-medical expenses are taxed as ordinary income (like a traditional IRA) but without penalty, making it a flexible supplemental retirement account. With decades of tax-free growth, an HSA funded at the family maximum annually can accumulate $500,000 or more by retirement.

A Donor Advised Fund (DAF) is a charitable giving vehicle authorized under IRC Section 4966 that allows you to make an irrevocable contribution, receive an immediate tax deduction in the year of the contribution, and then recommend grants to qualified charities over time. The DAF acts as a charitable investment account: you contribute cash, securities, or other assets, take the deduction upfront, and the funds grow tax-free until you direct them to specific charities. The most powerful tax application is bunching: instead of donating $10,000 per year and potentially falling below the standard deduction threshold, you contribute $50,000 in a single year to the DAF, claim the full itemized deduction that year, take the standard deduction in subsequent years, and distribute from the DAF to your chosen charities over the following five years. Contributing highly appreciated stock is especially advantageous because you deduct the full fair market value while completely avoiding capital gains tax on the appreciation under Section 170(e). We regularly structure DAF contributions for clients as part of year-end tax planning.

Amendments & Compliance

Amending a tax return makes sense whenever the expected refund or tax reduction meaningfully exceeds the cost and effort of filing the amendment. Common triggers include discovering missed deductions such as unreported business expenses, overlooked depreciation on rental properties, unclaimed education or energy credits, incorrect filing status, and missed retirement contribution deductions. Through our 3-Year Tax Lookback process, we have identified amendable errors on the majority of new client engagements, with recoverable refunds typically ranging from $5,000 to $80,000 across the three open years. It generally does not make sense to amend for immaterial amounts under $1,000, or for items that would likely trigger additional scrutiny on an otherwise clean return. For depreciation-related corrections, we often prefer filing Form 3115 to claim a catch-up adjustment in the current year rather than amending each prior year individually, which is administratively simpler and captures all missed depreciation in a single deduction.

The cost of filing an amended return varies based on complexity, but most amendments prepared by a qualified tax professional range from $500 to $2,500 per return. Simple amendments correcting a single missed deduction or credit are at the lower end, while amendments involving entity restructuring, multi-state adjustments, or complex depreciation corrections are at the higher end. At AE Tax Advisors, amended returns filed as part of the 3-Year Tax Lookback are included in the annual advisory engagement fee when they result from strategies identified during the planning process. We only recommend amendments when the expected refund substantially exceeds the preparation cost, and we provide a projected refund estimate before any filing work begins so clients can make an informed decision.

The IRS currently processes amended returns (Form 1040-X) in approximately 8 to 20 weeks from the date of receipt, though processing times can vary depending on IRS workload and the complexity of the changes. Electronically filed amendments, which became available in recent years, tend to process faster than paper-filed amendments. You can check the status of an amended return using the IRS "Where's My Amended Return?" tool at irs.gov beginning three weeks after filing. If the amendment results in a refund, the refund is issued after the processing is complete and is typically paid by direct deposit or check, depending on how the original return was filed. We track all filed amendments on behalf of our clients and follow up with the IRS if processing extends beyond the normal timeframe.

First-time penalty abatement (FTA) is an administrative waiver under IRS Internal Revenue Manual 20.1.1.3.6.1 that allows the IRS to remove failure-to-file, failure-to-pay, or failure-to-deposit penalties for a single tax period if the taxpayer has a clean compliance history. To qualify, you must have filed all currently required returns (or filed valid extensions), have no penalties for the three prior tax years (or any penalties were previously removed for reasonable cause), and have paid or arranged to pay any tax due. The FTA waiver can save thousands of dollars because IRS penalties are substantial: the failure-to-file penalty is 5% per month up to 25% of unpaid tax, and the failure-to-pay penalty is 0.5% per month. We proactively evaluate every client who receives an IRS penalty notice for FTA eligibility before exploring other abatement strategies such as reasonable cause. In many cases, a single phone call or letter to the IRS resolves the penalty entirely.

A properly prepared cost segregation study is designed to withstand IRS audit scrutiny. The IRS has published its own Cost Segregation Audit Techniques Guide (ATG), which outlines exactly what examiners look for, and a compliant study is prepared in accordance with those standards. Our studies include detailed engineering-based component analysis with line-item breakdowns, photographic documentation of reclassified components, citations to relevant IRC sections, Treasury Regulations, Revenue Rulings, and Tax Court precedent, and compliance with the IRS's principal elements outlined in the ATG. If audited, the study itself serves as the primary defense document. The IRS examiner will review the methodology, verify that reclassified components meet the criteria for their assigned recovery periods, and confirm that the taxpayer's return correctly reflects the study's conclusions. In our practice, properly prepared cost segregation studies have a strong track record under audit because the underlying methodology is well-established in tax law and supported by decades of Tax Court decisions.
