Cost Segregation for New Construction vs. Existing Properties

Published on May 27, 2026 | High-Intent Tax Strategy | AE Tax Advisors

For real estate investors building or buying properties, understanding cost segregation for new builds and existing properties is not optional -- it is essential for minimizing your tax burden legally and effectively. At AE Tax Advisors, we implement these strategies daily for our clients, and the results consistently demonstrate that proactive tax planning pays for itself many times over.

This guide provides a comprehensive overview of cost segregation for new builds and existing properties, including the key strategies, IRC citations, and practical implementation steps that separate theoretical knowledge from real-world tax savings.

Key Strategies and Considerations

  • New construction: engineering-based study using actual construction costs and blueprints
  • Existing properties: detailed engineering study with physical inspection and cost estimation
  • New construction advantages: precise cost records, architect/contractor documentation available
  • Existing property advantages: Form 3115 catch-up can produce larger single-year deductions
  • Renovation and improvement studies: segregating improvement costs from original building
  • Timing considerations: new construction placed-in-service date affects bonus depreciation

The IRC Framework

The strategies outlined above are grounded in established provisions of the Internal Revenue Code: IRC IRC Section 168 (MACRS property classifications), IRC Section 168(k) (bonus depreciation for qualifying property). These are not loopholes or aggressive positions -- they are provisions that Congress specifically enacted to encourage certain economic activities, and the IRS has published detailed guidance on their proper application.

Understanding how these provisions interact with each other is critical. For example, entity structuring for business owners decisions under IRC Section 7701 directly affect your qualification for the QBI deduction under IRC Section 199A, your self-employment tax under IRC Section 1402, and your passive activity classification under IRC Section 469. A change in one area ripples through the others, and optimizing the overall outcome requires modeling all interactions simultaneously.
