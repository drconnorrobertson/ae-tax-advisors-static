Airbnb & Short-Term Rental Tax Guide

Everything short-term rental owners need to know about taxation, deductions, material participation, and the strategies that turn your STR into a powerful tax shelter.

Request Your Free Tax Assessment

How Short-Term Rental Income Is Taxed

If you rent out a property on Airbnb, VRBO, or any other platform for an average stay of 7 days or fewer, the IRS treats your rental activity differently than a traditional long-term rental. This distinction is at the heart of the short-term rental tax strategy and creates opportunities that most property owners never realize they have.

Short-term rental income is reported on Schedule E of your personal tax return (or on the return of the entity that owns the property). The income itself is subject to federal income tax at your ordinary rates. However, unlike long-term rentals, STR income may also be subject to self-employment tax if you provide "substantial services" to your guests -- such as daily housekeeping, meals, or concierge services. If you simply provide the property and basic amenities, SE tax generally does not apply.

The more important distinction is how the IRS classifies your STR activity for purposes of the passive activity loss rules. Under IRC Section 469, most rental activities are automatically classified as passive -- meaning any losses can only offset other passive income. But short-term rentals with an average guest stay of 7 days or fewer are excluded from this automatic classification. This single rule creates the entire foundation for the STR loophole, one of the most valuable tax planning tools in the current code.

The STR Loophole Explained

The STR loophole works because of how three separate IRC provisions interact. First, IRC Section 469(j)(8) defines a "rental activity" as one where the average customer use is more than 7 days. A short-term rental with an average stay of 7 days or fewer does not meet this definition, so it is not automatically classified as passive.

Second, because the activity is not automatically passive, it can be classified as non-passive if you materially participate. Material participation is determined under the IRC Section 469 regulations, which provide seven tests. The most commonly used test for STR owners is the 100-hour test: you must participate in the activity for at least 100 hours during the year, and no other individual (including property managers) can participate more than you do.

Third, when the activity is classified as non-passive, any losses -- including paper losses created by depreciation -- can offset your active income. This is where cost segregation becomes transformative. A cost segregation study on a $400,000 STR property might generate $100,000 to $140,000 in first-year depreciation deductions through accelerated depreciation and bonus depreciation. If you materially participate in the property, that entire loss can offset your W-2 income, business income, or capital gains.

For a high-income earner in the 37% federal bracket, a $120,000 paper loss translates to approximately $44,000 in federal tax savings -- in a single year, from a single property. That is the power of the STR loophole when executed correctly.

Material Participation: Meeting the Requirements

Material participation is the gatekeeper for the STR loophole. If you do not materially participate, your STR losses remain non-passive by classification but cannot offset active income under the general passive activity rules. Meeting the material participation threshold is not difficult, but it must be documented properly.

The 100-hour test is the standard most STR owners use. Qualifying activities include managing bookings and guest communication, coordinating cleaning and maintenance, purchasing supplies and furnishings, handling accounting and bookkeeping, marketing the property, setting pricing, conducting property inspections, and performing or overseeing repairs and improvements. Most active STR owners comfortably exceed 100 hours per year per property.

The critical requirement that many owners overlook is the "more than any other individual" test. If you hire a property management company that logs more hours on your property than you do, you will fail the material participation test even if you personally exceed 100 hours. This does not mean you cannot use a property manager -- it means you need to structure the arrangement so that you retain primary involvement in key management activities and document your hours accordingly.

Documentation is essential. The IRS expects a contemporaneous log of your participation hours -- a record created at or near the time the work was performed, not reconstructed at year-end. We provide our clients with a simple tracking template and review their logs quarterly to ensure they are on pace to meet the requirements before the tax year ends.

Deductions Every STR Owner Should Claim

Short-term rental owners have access to a broad range of deductible expenses that directly reduce taxable income. Many owners miss legitimate deductions because they do not realize they qualify, or because their tax preparer is not familiar with STR-specific rules. Here are the key categories:

Operating expenses. Platform fees (Airbnb service charges, VRBO commissions), cleaning costs between guests, supplies (toiletries, linens, kitchen items), utilities, internet, cable, landscaping, pest control, HOA fees, and property insurance are all fully deductible against rental income.

Mortgage interest and property taxes. Interest on loans used to acquire or improve the property is deductible on Schedule E without the limitations that apply to personal residence mortgage interest. Property taxes are similarly deductible in full against rental income -- the $10,000 SALT cap does not apply to investment property taxes.

Repairs and maintenance. Routine repairs -- fixing a leaky faucet, replacing a broken appliance, repainting -- are deductible in the year they are incurred. Improvements that add value or extend the property's useful life must be capitalized and depreciated, but a cost segregation study can accelerate many of those deductions.

Travel expenses. If you travel to your STR property for management purposes, the travel costs -- airfare, mileage, lodging, and meals -- are deductible as business expenses. The IRS requires that the primary purpose of the trip be business-related, so keeping records of the management activities you performed during each visit is important.

Professional services. Fees paid to accountants, attorneys, property managers, photographers (for listing photos), and other professionals are fully deductible. So are costs for STR-specific software, pricing tools, and channel management platforms.

Furnishings and equipment. Furniture, appliances, electronics, outdoor equipment, hot tubs, and other tangible property placed in service at your STR can be depreciated -- and in many cases, fully expensed in the year of purchase under Section 179 or bonus depreciation. A cost segregation study identifies and classifies all of these items for maximum first-year deductions.

Cost Segregation for Short-Term Rentals

Cost segregation is the accelerator that makes the STR loophole so powerful. Without cost segregation, your property is depreciated over 39 years (the nonresidential recovery period that applies to most STR properties). With a cost segregation study, 25% to 35% of the property's cost basis is reclassified into 5-year, 7-year, and 15-year categories -- and under current bonus depreciation rules, those reclassified components can be written off immediately.

STR properties often produce higher cost segregation results than traditional rentals because they tend to be furnished, upgraded, and equipped with guest-facing amenities that qualify for shorter recovery periods. Hot tubs, outdoor kitchens, fire pits, specialized lighting, smart home systems, and hospitality-grade furnishings all fall into accelerated categories.

The combination of STR classification, material participation, and cost segregation creates a three-part framework that allows you to generate substantial paper losses in year one and apply those losses against your highest-taxed income. For investors acquiring their first STR, this framework often pays for the property's entire first-year carrying costs through tax savings alone.

State and Local Tax Considerations

Beyond federal taxes, STR owners must navigate a patchwork of state and local tax obligations. Most jurisdictions require STR operators to collect and remit occupancy taxes (also called lodging taxes, hotel taxes, or transient occupancy taxes). Rates vary widely -- from 2% to 15% or more depending on the jurisdiction. Some platforms like Airbnb collect and remit these taxes automatically in certain locations, but not all.

State income tax treatment of STR income and losses also varies. Some states conform to the federal passive activity rules, while others have their own classifications. A few states -- such as California -- have specific rules that limit the ability to use STR losses against other income at the state level even when the federal STR loophole applies.

Business licensing requirements, zoning restrictions, and local short-term rental permits add another layer of compliance. Operating without the required permits can result in fines, forced closure, and -- importantly for tax purposes -- the potential disallowance of deductions claimed on an unlicensed rental activity.
